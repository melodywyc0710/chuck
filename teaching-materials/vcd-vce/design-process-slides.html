Introduction
What is the Design Process?

The design process is the sequence of stages a designer moves through to take a problem from a client brief to a finished, resolved design.

It is not always linear — designers often loop back to earlier stages as ideas develop. But for study and folio purposes, it is broken into five key stages.

In your folio, you must show visible evidence of thinking at every stage — not just a final picture.

The 5 Stages
1Research & the Brief
2Generation of Ideas
3Development & Refinement
4Presentation
5Evaluation
Stage 1 of 5
Research & the Brief
Every design task starts with a design brief — a written statement from the client outlining the problem, purpose and requirements. Before generating ideas, a designer researches the context around the brief.
The Brief Must Identify
Client — who is commissioning the design
Target audience — who will use/view it
Purpose — what the design needs to achieve
Constraints — budget, timeline, materials, size
Exam vocab: brief, client, target audience, purpose, constraints, parameters, primary research, secondary research
Two Types of Research
Primary research
Information gathered first-hand — surveys, interviews, observation, site visits, photography.
Secondary research
Information gathered from existing sources — competitor designs, articles, market trends, mood boards.
Stage 2 of 5
Generation of Ideas
Once the brief is understood, the designer generates a wide range of possible solutions. Quantity over quality at this stage — the goal is to explore as many directions as possible.
Ideation Methods
Brainstorming — free listing of words/concepts related to the brief
Mind mapping — branching diagram connecting related ideas
Thumbnail sketches — small, quick, rough drawings exploring layout/form
SCAMPER / lateral thinking — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse
Exam vocab: ideation, thumbnail sketches, brainstorm, mind map, divergent thinking, concept
Stage 3 of 5
Development & Refinement
The strongest ideas from generation are selected and developed further — testing them against the brief, adding detail, and refining elements and principles until the concept is resolved.
What Happens at This Stage
Annotated drawings — sketches with written notes explaining decisions
Visualisation drawing — freehand, tonal renders exploring colour/material
Testing against the brief — does it meet the purpose and audience?
Feedback — peer or client feedback shapes further refinement
Exam vocab: refine, annotate, resolve, convergent thinking, iterate, visualisation drawing
Generation vs Development
Generation
Many ideas · rough · fast · divergent
Development
Fewer ideas · detailed · slower · convergent
Stage 4 of 5
Presentation
The final, resolved design is communicated clearly and professionally using presentation (technical) drawing methods so a client or manufacturer can understand it exactly.
Presentation Drawing Methods
Orthogonal drawing — accurate 2D views (plan, elevation)
Isometric drawing — 3D at fixed 30° angles, no perspective distortion
1-point & 2-point perspective — realistic 3D with vanishing points
Digital rendering — computer-generated final visuals
See the Technical Drawing handout for detailed diagrams of each method.
Presentation vs Visualisation Drawing
Visualisation — quick, freehand, used during the process to explore ideas.

Presentation — accurate, measured/rendered, used at the end to communicate the final design.
Stage 5 of 5
Evaluation
The final design is assessed against the original brief to determine whether it successfully solves the design problem for its intended audience and purpose.
Questions to Ask
Does it meet the purpose stated in the brief?
Is it appropriate for the target audience?
Were the constraints (budget, materials, size) respected?
What would you improve if given more time?
Exam vocab: evaluate, effective/ineffective, criteria, justify, refine further, meets the brief
The process is cyclical
Evaluation often leads back to further development or even new research — real design work loops back on itself rather than running in one straight line.

Exam Technique
Answering Design Process Questions
Stage → Purpose → Evidence
1. Name the stage of the process
2. Explain its purpose in that stage
3. Link to specific folio evidence (sketches, brief, feedback)
❌ Do NOT write:
"First I did research then I did some drawings."
✓ Write this instead:
"During the generation of ideas stage, thumbnail sketches were used to explore a wide range of layout options quickly, allowing divergent thinking before selecting a direction to develop further."
Sentence Starters
"The design brief specified..."
"During generation, [technique] was used to..."
"The idea was refined by..."
"Presentation drawing was used to communicate..."
"When evaluated against the brief, the design..."
Summary
The Design Process — Quick Reference
1. Research
Brief, client, audience, primary + secondary research.
2. Generation
Brainstorm, mind map, thumbnails. Quantity over quality.
3. Development
Annotate, visualise, test against the brief.
4. Presentation
Orthogonal, isometric, perspective, final render.
5. Evaluation
Assess against brief, audience, purpose, constraints.
💡 Reminder: the process is cyclical, not strictly linear — evaluation can send you back to any earlier stage.
