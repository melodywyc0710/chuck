🏛️ Bauhaus

Germany · 1919–1933
A German design school that unified fine art, craft, and industrial manufacturing under the philosophy: "form follows function." Bauhaus sought to make good design accessible to all through mass production.
  • Geometric, simplified forms
  • Primary colours (red, yellow, blue)
  • Sans-serif typography (Universal typeface)
  • Rejection of unnecessary ornament
  • Unity of art, craft, and technology
  • Grid-based composition
Key figures: Walter Gropius (founder), László Moholy-Nagy, Herbert Bayer, Marcel Breuer, Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky
Notable works: Breuer's Wassily Chair, Bayer's Universal typeface, Kandinsky's colour/form theory

🇨🇭 Swiss Style

Switzerland/Germany · 1950s–1970s
Also called the International Typographic Style. Developed in Zurich and Basel — emphasised mathematical grid systems, objective photography, and clean sans-serif typography to communicate universally.
  • Mathematical grid systems
  • Helvetica & Akzidenz-Grotesk typefaces
  • Flush-left, ragged-right text
  • Objective photography (not illustration)
  • Asymmetric, structured layouts
  • Neutral, universal communication
Key figures: Josef Müller-Brockmann, Armin Hofmann, Emil Ruder, Max Bill
Notable works: Müller-Brockmann's concert posters, NYC subway signage system, Helvetica typeface (1957)

🎨 Postmodernism

USA/Europe · 1980s–1990s
A reaction against the rigid rationalism of Modernism/Swiss Style. Postmodern designers deliberately broke the rules — mixing typefaces, layering images, using irony, historical references, and visual complexity.
  • Mixed typefaces and decorative fonts
  • Overlapping, layered compositions
  • Bright, clashing, unexpected colours
  • Historical references and pastiche
  • Irony, wit, and cultural commentary
  • No single "universal" design language
Key figures: David Carson, Paula Scher, Neville Brody, April Greiman, Wolfgang Weingart
Notable works: Ray Gun magazine (Carson), NYTM poster lettering (Scher), The Face magazine (Brody)

Discussing Design History in Exam Responses

How to connect a design to a historical movement

"This poster reflects the Swiss International Style through its use of a mathematical grid to organise information, Helvetica typeface for its neutral legibility, and objective photography in place of illustration. These choices prioritise clarity and universal communication consistent with the movement's philosophy."

Discussing influence across movements

"While the design draws on Bauhaus principles in its geometric, simplified forms and use of primary colour, the overlapping typography and mixed type sizes suggest a postmodern influence — using modernist visual language ironically rather than literally."
