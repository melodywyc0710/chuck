Design Principles

VCE Visual Communication Design · Unit 1 AOS 1 · VCAA 2024

VCAA-VCD-PRIN1

⚠️ VCAA 2024 — Know What's IN and What's OUT

  • NOT in VCAA 2024: Emphasis · Unity · Harmony · Rhythm · Dominance · Repetition (as a separate principle)
  • IN VCAA 2024: Balance · Contrast · Cropping · Scale · Proportion · Hierarchy · Pattern · Figure-Ground
✅ Always use ONLY the 8 principles listed above in your exam responses.

⚖️ Balance

The distribution of visual weight in a design. Can be symmetrical (mirror both sides), asymmetrical (different elements of equal weight), or radial (elements radiating from a centre).
e.g. A poster with one large image on the left balanced by several smaller text blocks on the right uses asymmetrical balance.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of balance is used through [symmetrical/asymmetrical/radial] arrangement, which creates a sense of [stability/dynamism] and ensures the viewer's eye..."

◐ Contrast

The juxtaposition of opposing visual qualities to create visual interest and emphasis. Can be tonal (light/dark), colour (complementary hues), scale (large/small), or texture (rough/smooth).
e.g. White text on a dark background creates high tonal contrast, making the text legible and dominant.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of contrast is achieved through [tonal/colour/scale/textural] opposition between [X] and [Y], which directs the viewer's attention to..."

✂️ Cropping

The selective framing of an image or subject to eliminate unwanted elements, create tension, direct focus, or imply continuation beyond the frame. Types: full, tight, partial, bleed.
e.g. Cropping a portrait so the top of the head is cut off creates an intimate, close-up feel that draws attention to the subject's eyes.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of cropping is used in this design by [framing/eliminating/cutting] the [subject/image], which creates a sense of [tension/intimacy/focus] and..."

🔍 Scale

The relative size of elements in relation to each other or to the real world. Exaggerated scale creates emphasis; realistic scale creates clarity; distorted scale creates surreal or dramatic effects.
e.g. A large product image next to small text creates visual dominance through scale, establishing the product as the focus.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of scale is evident through the [enlarged/reduced] size of [element], which [emphasises/diminishes] it in relation to [other elements], communicating..."

📏 Proportion

The size relationship between parts of an object, or between an object and the whole design. Linked to the golden ratio (1:1.618). Proportional relationships convey harmony, anatomy, and aesthetic appeal.
e.g. The golden ratio is used in logo design to ensure letterforms are proportionally pleasing and balanced.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of proportion is demonstrated through the [size relationship/ratio] of [X to Y], which creates a sense of [harmony/tension/realism] in the design."

🏆 Hierarchy

The organisation of visual elements by importance to guide the viewer's reading order. Achieved through size, weight, colour, position, and typeface variation.
e.g. A headline in large bold type, followed by a subheading, then body text, creates a clear visual hierarchy that guides the eye top to bottom.
Exam sentence starter
"Visual hierarchy is established through the use of [larger/bolder/coloured] type for [heading], which signals that this information has greater visual priority than [body text/captions]."

🔁 Pattern

The repetition of visual elements (shapes, colours, lines, textures) in a regular or irregular arrangement, creating rhythm, texture, and unity. Distinguished from the (non-VCAA) "repetition" principle.
e.g. A repeating chevron motif across the background of a brochure creates visual texture and reinforces brand identity.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of pattern is created through the repetition of [element], which generates visual [rhythm/texture/unity] and reinforces the [theme/brand identity]."

◐ Figure-Ground

The relationship between the subject (figure) and background (ground). Can be stable (clear separation) or ambiguous (reversible). Negative space can become a meaningful figure.
e.g. The FedEx logo uses the negative space between 'E' and 'x' to create an arrow — the ground becomes a meaningful figure.
Exam sentence starter
"The principle of figure-ground is used through the [contrast/negative space/colour] which separates [subject] from [background], directing the viewer's attention to [focal point]."
