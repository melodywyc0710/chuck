Introduction
What are Design Principles?

Design principles are the rules and guidelines that govern how design elements are arranged and combined.

If elements are the "what", principles are the "how" — how elements are organised to communicate effectively.

Elements + Principles together = a complete visual analysis vocabulary for the VCE exam.

The 8 VCAA 2024 Principles
1. Balance
2. Contrast
3. Cropping
4. Scale
5. Proportion
6. Hierarchy
7. Pattern
8. Figure-Ground
⚠️
NOT in VCAA 2024
These principles are often taught but are NOT part of the VCAA 2024 study design:
Emphasis
Unity
Harmony
Rhythm
Dominance
Using these in the exam will cost you marks. Stick to the 8 official VCAA 2024 principles.
Principle 1 of 8
Balance
The distribution of visual weight across a design. Balance creates stability or intentional tension and affects how the viewer perceives the composition.
Three Types
Symmetrical — mirrored halves. Formal, stable, trustworthy.
Asymmetrical — different elements, equal visual weight. Dynamic, modern, interesting.
Radial — elements radiate from a central point. Energy, movement, focus.
Exam vocab: symmetrical, asymmetrical, radial, visual weight, equilibrium, formal, informal, off-centre
Principle 2 of 8
Contrast
The juxtaposition of opposing visual qualities to create visual interest, emphasis and separation between elements. Contrast draws the viewer's eye.
Types of Contrast
Tonal contrast — light vs dark
Colour contrast — complementary colours, warm vs cool
Scale contrast — large vs small elements
Texture contrast — rough vs smooth
Typographic contrast — bold vs light, serif vs sans-serif
Exam vocab: high contrast, low contrast, juxtaposition, opposition, visual tension, emphasis, differentiation
Principle 3 of 8
Cropping
How much of a subject or image is shown within the frame. Cropping controls what the viewer sees, directs focus and creates compositional interest.
Effect of Cropping
Tight crop — creates intimacy, drama, focus on detail
Wide crop — shows context and environment
Partial crop — implies the subject extends beyond the frame, creates energy
Rule of thirds — off-centre cropping creates visual interest
Exam vocab: tight crop, wide shot, close-up, rule of thirds, frame, bleed, partial, compositional, foreground, background
Principle 4 of 8
Scale
The size of an element relative to other elements in the design. Scale creates visual hierarchy, depth, drama and communicates relative importance.
Scale vs Proportion
Scale = size of one element compared to another element
Proportion = relationship between parts within the same element
Large scale — importance, dominance, closeness
Small scale — distance, subordination, delicacy
Exaggerated scale — drama, surrealism, visual impact
Exam vocab: large-scale, small-scale, relative size, dominance, exaggerated, monumental, intimate, depth, hierarchy
Principle 5 of 8
Proportion
The relationship between the parts of a design or between a part and the whole. Good proportion creates harmony; distorted proportion creates unease or emphasis.
Key Ideas
Golden ratio (φ = 1:1.618) — mathematically harmonious proportion found in nature and classical design
Rule of thirds — dividing composition into thirds creates natural proportion
Distorted proportion — used deliberately for emphasis, satire or surrealism
Page proportion — margins, columns and gutters in layout design
Exam vocab: golden ratio, harmonious, distorted, exaggerated, relative, balanced, classical, natural proportion
Principle 6 of 8
Hierarchy
The visual organisation of elements by order of importance. Hierarchy guides the viewer's eye through the design in a deliberate sequence.
How to Create Hierarchy
Size — larger = more important
Weight — bold attracts before light
Colour — saturated/bright draws eye first
Position — top-left is read first in Western convention
White space — isolation gives importance
Contrast — most contrasting element is seen first
Exam vocab: visual hierarchy, primary, secondary, tertiary, reading order, dominant, subordinate, entry point
Typographic Hierarchy Example
HEADING
Subheading level two
Body text at third level — readable but subordinate to the headings above. Guides the reader through information.

Principle 7 of 8
Pattern
The repetition of a visual element (shape, line, colour, texture) to create visual cohesion, rhythm, texture and decoration.
Key Ideas
Regular pattern — predictable repetition, creates order and calm
Random pattern — irregular repetition, feels organic and natural
Pattern break — interrupting a pattern draws immediate attention
Cultural patterns — indigenous, geometric, motif-based carry meaning
💡 Exam tip: Pattern can be created by any element — repeated lines, dots, shapes or colours.
Principle 8 of 8
Figure-Ground
The perceptual relationship between a subject (figure) and its background (ground). The viewer's brain naturally separates figure from ground to find meaning.
Key Concepts
Figure — the subject, appears to come forward
Ground — the background, appears to recede
Stable — clear separation (figure reads easily)
Ambiguous/Reversible — both figure and ground compete (e.g. Rubin's Vase) — used deliberately for engagement
Exam vocab: figure, ground, positive space, negative space, stable, ambiguous, reversible, foreground, background, depth
Exam Technique
Analysing Principles in the Exam
Principle → Use → Effect on Audience
1. Name the principle
2. Describe how it's applied in the design
3. Explain the effect on the target audience
❌ Do NOT write:
"The design has good balance and looks nice."
✓ Write this instead:
"Asymmetrical balance is achieved through a large product image on the left counterweighted by dense typographic content on the right, creating a dynamic yet stable composition that prevents the design from feeling static."
Sentence Starters
"Hierarchical contrast is established through..."
"The use of asymmetrical balance creates..."
"Scale contrast between [X] and [Y] conveys..."
"The figure-ground relationship is [stable/ambiguous], which..."
"Through tight cropping of the [subject], the designer..."
"Repetition of [X] creates a pattern that..."
Summary
All 8 Principles — Quick Reference
Balance
Distribution of visual weight. Symmetrical, asymmetrical or radial.
Contrast
Opposition of visual qualities. Draws the eye, creates emphasis.
Cropping
How much is shown in the frame. Controls focus and energy.
Scale
Relative size between elements. Creates hierarchy and depth.
Proportion
Relationship between parts of an element. Harmony or distortion.
Hierarchy
Ordering by importance. Guides reading order through the design.
Pattern
Repetition of elements. Creates texture, rhythm and cohesion.
Figure-Ground
Subject vs background relationship. Stable or ambiguous.
⚠️ Reminder: Emphasis, Unity, Harmony, Rhythm and Dominance are NOT in VCAA 2024. Do not use them in exam responses.
