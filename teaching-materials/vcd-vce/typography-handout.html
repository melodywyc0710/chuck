2. Typeface Classifications
Aa

Serif

Small strokes at letter ends. Traditional, authoritative, trustworthy.

Times New Roman, Garamond, Georgia
Use: books, newspapers, formal docs
Aa

Sans-Serif

No strokes at ends. Modern, clean, accessible.

Helvetica, Arial, Futura, Gill Sans
Use: screens, signage, modern brands
Aa

Script / Decorative

Mimics handwriting. Expressive, personal — display use only.

Brush Script, display fonts
Use: invitations, titles (not body!)
Aa

Monospaced

Each character = equal width. Technical, precise.

Courier, Consolas, Space Mono
Use: code, forms, typewriter look
3. Spacing: Kerning · Tracking · Leading

Kerning

AV vs AV

Adjusts space between two specific characters. Fixes optically uneven pairs like AV, WA, To, Ty. Applied to display/heading text.

Tracking

LOOSE TIGHT

Adjusts space across a range of characters (word, line, or paragraph). Loose = airy/luxury; tight = dense/urgent.

Leading

Tight line spacing
feels cramped here
Loose leading
feels airy here

Vertical space between lines of text. Named after lead strips in manual typesetting. 120%–150% of font size is standard for body text.

4. Typographic Hierarchy & Exam Technique

Creating Hierarchy Through:

ToolHow it works
SizeLarger = higher priority; draws eye first
WeightBold/black weight dominates lighter weights
ColourHigh-contrast or saturated colour = emphasis
PositionTop/centre = primary; bottom/edge = secondary
TypefaceContrasting classifications (serif + sans) signal levels
